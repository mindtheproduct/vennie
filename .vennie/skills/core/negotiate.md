# Negotiation Preparation

Help the user prepare for a high-stakes negotiation — salary, title, scope, role change. This is where career evidence turns into leverage.

## How to Start

"What are you negotiating? Salary, title, scope, a new role, or something else?"

Then understand the full picture:
- "What's your current situation? (Title, comp, scope)"
- "What are you asking for specifically?"
- "When is this conversation happening?"
- "Who are you negotiating with? What do you know about their style?"

## Build the Evidence Base

Pull from everything available:

**Career Evidence (`06-Evidence/`):**
- Quantified wins — these are your strongest cards
- Scope expansion evidence — "I'm already doing the job"
- Impact metrics — revenue, users, efficiency, quality
- Stakeholder praise — external validation

**Decision History (`03-Decisions/`):**
- High-stakes decisions you made and their outcomes
- Shows judgment and ownership

**Market Signals:**
- "Do you have market data? Levels.fyi, salary surveys, recruiter conversations?"
- "Have you received competing offers or recruiter outreach?"
- If they have a specific company/role in mind, help frame the comparison

## Build the Narrative

A negotiation is a story with three chapters:

**Chapter 1: What I've Delivered**
- Lead with metrics. Numbers beat narratives.
- Connect individual contributions to business outcomes
- Show trajectory — not just what you did, but the upward arc

**Chapter 2: What I'm Worth**
- Market positioning — where you sit relative to peers
- Unique value — what makes you hard to replace
- Scope reality — if you're operating above your level, document it

**Chapter 3: What I'm Asking For**
- Specific, clear ask (not "I want more money" — "I'm looking for a base of $X")
- Anchor high but reasonable
- Have a walk-away number (even if you don't share it)
- Think beyond base: equity, title, scope, flexibility, development budget

## Rehearsal

Play the other side. Ask:

"Your manager might respond with [objection]. How would you handle that?"

Common objections to practice:
- "The budget isn't there right now"
- "You haven't been in the role long enough"
- "We need to see more [X] before promoting"
- "Everyone is underpaid, not just you"
- "Let's revisit in 6 months"

For each, help them craft a response that's firm but not adversarial.

**If a "My Boss" custom persona exists:** Use it for ultra-realistic rehearsal. Mirror the manager's known communication style, priorities, and likely pushback points.

## The Prep Document

Generate a cheat sheet they can reference before the conversation:

```markdown
# Negotiation Prep: [Context]
**Date of conversation:** [date]
**With:** [person]

## My Ask
[Specific, clear statement]

## My Evidence (Top 5)
1. [Strongest win with metric]
2. [Second strongest]
3. [Third]
4. [Fourth]
5. [Fifth]

## My Market Position
[Brief market context]

## Likely Objections & Responses
| They might say... | I'll respond with... |
|---|---|
| ... | ... |

## My Walk-Away
[What's the minimum acceptable outcome? — for my eyes only]

## Tone Notes
[How to stay composed, key phrases to use/avoid]
```

## Key Principles to Reinforce

- **Never negotiate against yourself.** State your ask and wait.
- **Silence is a tool.** After making your case, stop talking.
- **Be specific.** "I'd like to discuss my compensation" is weak. "I'm looking for a base of $X based on [evidence]" is strong.
- **Separate the relationship from the negotiation.** You can be warm AND firm.
- **Get it in writing.** Verbal agreements evaporate.

## Saving

- Save prep document to `06-Evidence/Negotiations/YYYY-MM-DD-[context].md`
- After the negotiation: prompt to capture the outcome and any follow-up items

## End With

"You're prepared. The three things to remember walking in: [1] lead with [strongest evidence], [2] make your specific ask early, [3] when you've made your case, stop talking. You've earned this. Go get it."
