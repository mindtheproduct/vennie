# Interview Preparation

Turn interview anxiety into interview confidence. Research the company, map your stories to likely questions, and practice.

## How to Start

"What company and role? Got a URL or description?"

Get:
- Company name and what they do
- Role title and level
- Job posting (if available)
- Interview stage (phone screen, technical, behavioral, final round?)
- Who you're meeting with (if known)

## Company Research

Gather intelligence:

**From their website:**
- What's the product? Who uses it?
- Recent launches, blog posts, press releases
- Company values / mission (look for substance, not fluff)

**From public sources:**
- G2, Glassdoor reviews (product and company)
- Recent news, funding rounds, leadership changes
- Competitor landscape — who are they fighting?

**From the job posting:**
- What problems are they hiring for? (Read between the lines)
- Tech stack, tools, methodologies mentioned
- Team size and reporting structure clues

Synthesize into a brief:

```markdown
## Company Brief: [Company]
**Product:** [what they do, for whom]
**Stage:** [startup/growth/enterprise]
**Recent news:** [1-2 relevant items]
**Their challenges:** [what you can infer they're dealing with]
**Competitors:** [key competitors]
**Why this matters for you:** [connection to your experience]
```

## Question Preparation

### Likely Questions

Generate questions based on:
- Role level and type (PM, engineer, designer, leader)
- Company stage (startup questions differ from enterprise)
- Job posting requirements

For each likely question:
1. **The question** — phrased how they'd actually ask it
2. **Your best story** — mapped from `06-Evidence/` and `04-Projects/`
3. **Key points to hit** — metrics, decisions, outcomes
4. **Watch out for** — common pitfalls with this question

### Story Bank

Build a story bank from evidence:

| Story | Best For | Key Metric |
|-------|----------|------------|
| [Project/Win] | [Question type: leadership, technical, conflict, etc.] | [Number] |

Use the STAR format internally (Situation, Task, Action, Result) but deliver stories naturally — nobody likes an answer that sounds like a template.

### Questions to ASK Them

Prepare 5-7 questions that show you've done your homework:
- Questions about their specific challenges (not generic "what's the culture like")
- Questions about the role's real scope and autonomy
- Questions about what success looks like in 6 months
- One question that shows you've researched them specifically

"The quality of your questions tells them more about you than your answers do."

## Mock Interview (Optional)

If the user wants to practice:

"Want to do a mock round? I'll ask questions and give you honest feedback."

- Ask questions in interview style
- After each answer, give specific feedback:
  - "Good — the metric at the end was strong. But you buried the lead. Start with the outcome."
  - "That was too long. The core of your answer was the third sentence. Lead with it."
  - "You didn't mention the impact. Always land the plane — what happened as a result?"

Be tough but constructive. A gentle mock interview is useless.

## Saving

- Save prep to `06-Evidence/Interview_Prep/YYYY-MM-DD-[company].md`
- Save company brief separately if useful for future reference

## End With

"You're prepped. Three things to remember: [1] Lead with your [strongest story] when they ask about [likely topic]. [2] Ask about [specific company challenge] — it shows you've done homework. [3] The first 5 minutes set the tone — be warm, be specific, be you. Good luck."
