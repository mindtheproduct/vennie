# Quick Challenge

Fast, punchy adversarial analysis. No ceremony — just the counter-arguments.

## How It Works

Look at the user's last statement, decision, or plan. In 3-5 bullet points, give the strongest counter-arguments. Each bullet should be specific and actionable, not vague hand-waving.

## Format

**Challenging: "[restate their position in one line]"**

- [Counter-argument 1 — the most obvious hole]
- [Counter-argument 2 — the thing they're probably underweighting]
- [Counter-argument 3 — the stakeholder or user perspective they haven't considered]
- [Counter-argument 4 — the timing or resource issue, if relevant]
- [Counter-argument 5 — the second-order effect, if relevant]

Still confident? If yes, you've stress-tested it. If not, let's dig deeper with `/red-team`.

## Rules

- Be specific. "This might not work" is worthless. "This assumes your sales team can close enterprise deals with a 2-week cycle, but your current average is 6 weeks" is useful.
- No more than 5 bullets. Constraint forces you to pick the strongest arguments, not the most.
- No more than 2-3 sentences per bullet. Brevity is the point.
- Don't hedge. If you think it's a bad call, say so directly in the bullet.
- Reference vault data if you have it — past decisions, person context, project history. Grounded challenges hit harder than theoretical ones.
- End with the `/red-team` escape hatch every time.

## If the User Says "Still Confident"

Good. Move on. The challenge did its job — it either strengthened their conviction or surfaced a doubt worth exploring. Either outcome is valuable.

## If the User Says "Actually..."

Transition naturally into a deeper discussion. You don't need to invoke `/red-team` formally — just keep pulling the thread.
